Help With Classes

Math Classes

Engineering requires taking a lot of math classes, especially if you were to get a math minor. Therefore, it is important that you take advantage of the math help sessions that are available for specific math classes which can be found on the main math website. Of course, your professors and tutoring outside of school are good resources too, but remember this is free! The math website is math.tamu.edu


Tutoring


Professors

This is a good and free resource that should be taken advantage of! Although it may seem intimidating at first, it is always good to ask your professors for help because they get to see the effort you put into their class. Although it may not always happen, professors use that as a deciding factor for bumping a letter grade or simply helping you more as they see the amount of effort you put into understanding the material of the course.

Teacher Assistants (T.A.s)

Although not all classes have T.A.s, you will find that most of the biggest classes will have at least one. They are there not only to help professors grade papers, but also to help students with the course material. There will be many times that the professors will not be available and this is when T.A.s can become helpful as they may be more available than the professors. Going to them for help is free and it is highly encouraged to ask them for help.

Outside of School

There are many other resources outside of school that students take advantage of, but are costly. There are more tutoring locations for the bigger classes such as the more general classes that all engineers take. Some of those locations include “4.0” and “A+” among others. There are also private tutors who are seen advertised in billboards throughout the engineering buildings, so just make sure to keep an eye on the flyers that you see posted if you are interested.

Academic/Industry Experience

Study Abroad

Studying abroad is a great opportunity to study in a different country for some time. It’s a good resume builder and the experience is unforgettable! Don’t be too scared to take the opportunity to travel while continuing your education. Many students regret not studying abroad so don’t make the same mistake!

How can I study abroad?

There are various ways that you could study abroad. You have a choice to take classes under your major (that information can be found in your major’s website). You can also take classes outside your major that could possibly be used as electives. To find out if those electives count towards your major’s curriculum, you would have to take to your academic advisor.

Getting an internship may be difficult when you are a freshman. However, it is still possible to get one! Having an internship is good experience to maturity and knowing how the “real world” works. Jobs

Many Texas A&M students need to work while going to school. Although it may be difficult to manage a job with classes, it is possible. It is recommended to find jobs that are related to your field of study. Anything else is still good experience but working with something related to your field will be more beneficial for building your resume. The job search intensifies upon graduation. Don’t wait too long and start looking for a job BEFORE you graduate. This increases your chances of getting a job and you don’t want to wait too long to find that you are job-less upon graduation. Once again, take advantage of resources and use the HireAggies website for your job and internship search.

Aerospace Engineering

Aerospace engineering is a complex, rapidly changing field whose primary application is the design and development of flight vehicles such as aircraft, missiles, spacecraft and satellites. Aerospace engineering is also important and applicable to other vehicles and systems such as submarines, automobiles, trucks and rapid transit, and can include advanced robotics, exotic materials and computational simulations.

Student Organizations: Sigma Gamma Tau (Aerospace Engineering Honor Society), AAWE (Aggie Aerospace Women in Engineering), AggieSat Lab (Student Satellite Program), AHS (American Helicopter Society), AIAA (American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics), HABC (High Altitude Balloon Club), SFTE (Society of Flight Test Engineers), SRT (Sounding Rocketry Team), SEDS (Students for the Exploration and Development of Space).

For more information, visit engineering.tamu.edu/aerospace

Biological & Agricultural Engineering

The Biological and Agricultural Engineering Department provides quality education, research and outreach in engineering and technology for the world’s agricultural, biological, environmental, and food systems. Biological and agricultural engineers apply their knowledge of physical and biological sciences, mathematics, engineering principles, and engineering design to the production and processing of food and fiber, to the preservation of environmental quality, to biological systems and processes, and to machine systems that interface with all of these. Graduates are successfully employed in engineering jobs in industry, in government and in academia. Recent employers include environmental consulting firms, equipment manufacturers, crop storage and handling industries, the cotton and forest products industries, food and feed processing industries, concentrated animal production industries, biotechnology companies, chemical companies, and governmental agencies.

Student Organizations: AGSM (Agricultural Systems Management Club), ASABE (American Society of Agricultural & Biological Engineers).

For more information: visit baen.tamu.edu

Biomedical Engineering

Biomedical engineering is a discipline that integrates engineering principles and the study of the life sciences and medicine. Students in biomedical engineering develop engineering strategies and scientific principles, and the methods for developing bioinstrumentation, biomaterials, diagnostic and therapeutic devices, and other technologies needed in medicine and life sciences.

Student Organizations: Alpha Eta Mu Beta (Biomedical Engineering Honor Society), BMES (Biomedical Engineering Society), EWH (Engineering World Health), SFB (Society for Biomaterials), SPE (Society of Plastics Engineers), SPIE(The International Society for Optics and Photonics).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/biomedical

Chemical Engineering

Chemical engineers use chemistry, physics, biology, math, and other sciences along with engineering principles to make better products such as high-efficiency fuels, medicine, electronics, food and cosmetics. Everything we use involves chemistry, and chemical engineering touches just about every part of our lives. If you put on soft contact lenses, changed into sweat-proof athletic wear, filled your car with gasoline before driving to the gym, and drink a plastic bottle of purified water or artificially sweetened soda after your workout, you can thank chemical engineers for making those products possible. Chemical engineers design processes and products to solve problems and to supply vital materials for our technology-based society. Their work ranges from making clean energy, to producing more-affordable medicine, to streamlining semiconductor manufacturing – and even ways to improve food production and processing.

Student Organizations: Omega Chi Epsilon (Chemical Engineering Honor Society), AIChE (American Institute of Chemical Engineers), ChEGSA (Chemical Engineering Graduate Student Association).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/chemical

Civil Engineering

The Zachry Department of Civil Engineering offers students the option to specialize their education in seven of specialties. Students who pursue the general civil engineering option take breadth courses in environmental engineering, engineering measurements (surveying), transportation, water resources, materials of construction, geotechnical engineering, structural analysis, design of steel and concrete structures and project management. Focus courses can be selected in the areas listed above or in public works, urban planning, highway design, hydrology, environmental issues and/or public safety and health.

Student Organizations: Chi Epsilon (Civil Engineering Honor Society), ASCE (American Society of Civil Engineers), AWRA (American Water Resources Association), AEES (Association of Environmental Engineering Students), CMSO (Civil Materials Student Organization), TAMU G-I (Texas A&M Student Chapter of the GeoInstitute), ITE (Institute of Transportation Engineers), MTS & SNAME (Marine Technology Society/Society of Naval Architects and Marine Engineers), SEAoT (Structural Engineers Association of Texas)

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/civil

Computer Science & Engineering

The computer science curriculum includes a sound preparation in science, mathematics, English, statistics and computing. Electives are available in the areas of: algorithms, languages and computability, software systems, networking, computer systems and architecture, artificial intelligence and cognitive modeling, vision, graphics and robotics and computational science and engineering. The computer engineering curriculum provides a balanced view of hardware, software, hardware-software tradeoffs, analysis, design and implementation techniques. It is a dynamic and broadly interdisciplinary field that continues to experience rapid professional growth. The curriculum includes computer networks, computer architecture, artificial microcomputers, VLSI and large scale hardware and software systems.

Student Organizations: Upsilon Pi Epsilon (Computer Science Honor Society), AWICS (Aggie Women in Computer Science), CSEGSA (Computer Science Graduate Student Association), TAMUhack, TACS (Texas A&M Computing Society), TAGD (Texas Aggie Game Developers).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/cse

Electrical & Computer Engineering

Almost any technology that distinguishes the 20th and 21st centuries from previous history has the imprint of electrical & computer engineering – electric power, radio, television, radar, satellite communication, global positioning system, medical diagnostic and procedure systems, sophisticated domestic appliances, cell phones, computers and sophisticated sensors and control systems used in under water, space exploration and national security. Electrical engineering has indeed advanced national and global prosperity by research, development and application of electrical and information technologies and sciences for the benefit of humanity and has helped create the global village. By choosing electrical or computer engineering our graduates embark on an exciting and productive career and help in shaping a better future for mankind. Areas of Specialization are Analog & Mixed Signal; Biomedical Imaging & Genomic Signal Processing; Computer Engineering; Control Systems; E/M & Microwaves; Electric Power & Electronics; Solid State Electronics; Photonics & Nano-Engineering; and Telecommunications & Signal Processing.

Student Organizations: Eta Kappa Nu (Electrical & Computer Engineering Honor Society), IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers), IEEE PELS/IAS/PES (IEEE-Power & Energy-Industry Application-Power Electronics Societies).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/electrical

Engineering Technology & Industrial Distribution

Engineering Technology (ET) combines scientific and engineering knowledge and methods with technical skills to put engineering design to work in production and manufacturing. Education in engineering technology focuses on the applied aspect of science and engineering rather than the theoretical and analytical aspects. The Industrial Distribution Program develops leaders for Sales Engineering, Sales Management, Logistics and Warehouse/Inventory Management, Consulting, Manufacturer, Distributor, Branch Management, Purchasing and mid-management positions with manufacturers who sell through distributors.

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/etid

Industrial & Systems Engineering

Industrial engineering is an engineering discipline devoted to the design, installation, improvement and control of integrated systems of people, materials, and facilities in a wide range or organizations that produce goods or render services. Like other engineering fields, industrial engineering is concerned with solving problems through the application of specialized knowledge in mathematics and science, as well as the principles of engineering. An important characteristic of industrial engineering is its system approach to integrate the basic resources of production and service systems and other relevant resources, such as information and energy, in such a way as to create a smooth, efficient and competitive operation within an enterprise. Industrial and systems engineers are needed in virtually all types of enterprises, ranging from industries such as manufacturing, distribution, logistics, transportation, and construction; service sectors such as health care, retail, banking, and engineering consulting to government agencies, military, and non-profit organizations.

Student Organizations: Alpha Pi Mu (Industrial and Systems Engineering Honor Society), IIE (Institute of Industrial Engineers), INFORMS (Institute for Operations Research and the Management Sciences).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/industrial

Mechanical Engineering

Mechanical Engineering is one of the oldest engineering disciplines, having culminated from the industrial revolution of the 19th century. Traditionally concerned with harnessing and converting energy forms, mechanical engineers are inherently multi-disciplinary. For example, the design of a car engine combines the fields of chemistry, thermodynamics, heat transfer, fluid dynamics, electronic controls, dynamics and vibrations, materials science, and mechanical design. Although the mechanical engineer may specialize in one of these fields, a cursory knowledge of each of these fields is necessary to interact with other engineers.

Student Organizations: Pi Tau Sigma (Mechanical Engineering Honor Society), ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers), ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers), SAE (Society of Automotive Engineers).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/mechanical

Nuclear Engineering

Nuclear engineering deals with the application and utilization of nuclear processes and radiations. The use of nuclear energy for the production of electrical power is a mature industry. Nuclear engineers work on all aspects of the nuclear fuel cycle and for many different types of employers such as government and private labs, regulatory agencies, reactor vendors, utilities and architect engineers. In addition, nuclear energy for space applications is a rapidly expanding field. Radionuclide technology in industry and medicine requires a large number of well-trained radiological health engineers.

Student Organizations: Alpha Nu Sigma (Nuclear Engineering Honor Society), ANS (American Nuclear Society), HPS(Health Physics Society), INMM (Institute of Nuclear Materials Management), WiN (Women in Nuclear)

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/nuclear

Ocean Engineering

Ocean engineering is the application of engineering principles in an ocean environment. The diversity of this environment and the challenge of the ocean frontier make it an exciting career for young ocean engineers. Ocean engineering prepares people to build systems that monitor and control coastal environments. Students learn to build dikes, flood control systems, and power stations in or near the ocean. They also learn to use math and science to solve problems caused by tidal forces, waves, and currents.

Student Organizations: Omega Epsilon (Ocean Engineering Honor Society), SNAME (Society of Naval Architects and Marine Engineers), MTS (Marine Technology Society).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/ocean

Petroleum Engineering

Petroleum Engineering is primarily concerned with the economic extraction of oil, gas, and other natural resources form the earth. Producing oil and gas is accomplished through the design, drilling and operation of wells and well systems, and the integrated management of the underground reservoirs in which the resources are found. The goal of the TAMU petroleum engineering curriculum is to provide a modern engineering education with balance between fundamentals and practice, and to graduate engineers prepared for life-long learning but capable of being productive contributors immediately. Emphasis is on mathematics, computer applications, communication skills and interdisciplinary problem solving.

Student Organizations: Pi Epsilon Tau (Petroleum engineering Honor Society), SPE (Society of Petroleum Engineers), AADE (American Association of Drilling Engineers).

For more information: visit engineering.tamu.edu/petroleum
